
## Complete Guide to Nobility Titles

A comprehensive reference of noble ranks and their responsibilities, organized by hierarchical rank.

---- 

## Rank 1: Monarch

**King/Queen**

- Supreme ruler, head of state, ultimate authority over the realm, military commander, and lawgiver

---- 

## Rank 2: Royal Family

**Prince/Princess** (English/European)

- Royal family members (children of the monarch), governed provinces or had ceremonial roles

**Infante/Infanta** (Spanish/Portuguese)

- Title for royal children who weren’t the heir to the throne

**Archduke** (Austrian Habsburg)

- Special princely title unique to Austrian Habsburgs, ranking above regular dukes

**Prince-Bishop** (Holy Roman Empire)

- Ecclesiastical rulers who held both religious authority as bishops AND secular power as princes, governing territories

---- 

## Rank 3: Duke

**Duke/Duchess** (English/European)

- Highest rank of nobility below royalty
- Governed large territories (duchies), commanded armies, administered justice, and advised the crown

---- 

## Rank 4: March/Border Lords

**Marquess/Marchioness** (English)

- Originally military commanders of border territories (marches)
- Protected frontier regions and maintained order in strategic areas

**Margrave/Margravine** (German)

- Guarded the “marks” (marches/borders) of the Holy Roman Empire
- Brandenburg was a famous margraviate

---- 

## Rank 5: Count/Earl

**Earl** (English) / **Count/Countess** (Continental European)

- Governed counties (shires), collected taxes, raised troops, administered local justice, and managed royal estates in their region

**Greve** (Scandinavian)

- Scandinavian equivalent of a count/earl

**Jarl** (Norse/Scandinavian)

- Powerful chieftains who ruled regions, led warriors, and acted as the king’s deputies
- Some jarls were nearly as powerful as kings

**Riksjarl** (Nordic)

- “Realm Jarl” - the highest jarl who acted as the king’s chief advisor and sometimes regent

**Landgrave** (German)

- “Land count” ruling a larger territory than a regular count
- Ranked between count and duke in German states

---- 

## Rank 6: Special Counts

**Count Palatine/Pfalzgraf** (Holy Roman Empire)

- Special counts with royal privileges who could act with the king’s authority
- Could judge in the king’s name and even mint coins

**Burgrave** (German)

- “Castle count” who governed a fortified town or castle
- Responsible for its defense and the surrounding territory

---- 

## Rank 7: Viscount

**Viscount/Viscountess** (English/European)

- Originally deputies to earls/counts
- Handled administrative duties, collected revenues, and managed smaller territories

---- 

## Rank 8: Baron

**Baron/Baroness** (English/European)

- Lowest rank of major nobility
- Held land directly from the king, provided military service, managed estates, and served in local governance

**Friherre** (Swedish/Norwegian/Danish)

- Scandinavian equivalent of a baron (literally “free lord”)

**Boyar** (Slavic/Russian)

- Slavic nobility forming an aristocratic council
- Owned vast estates with serfs

**Voivode** (Eastern European)

- Military commander and provincial governor in Romanian, Serbian, and Polish territories
- Sometimes semi-autonomous rulers

---- 

## Rank 9: Baronet

**Baronet** (English)

- Hereditary title below barons (not always considered full nobility)
- Landed gentry who could pass their title to heirs

---- 

## Rank 10: Knights and Warrior Nobility

**Knight** (European)

- Lowest rank often included in nobility
- Provided military service, protected the realm, and managed smaller estates
- Not always hereditary

**Banneret** (European)

- Higher grade of knight who could lead troops under his own banner
- Ranked between knight and baron

**Thegn/Thane** (Anglo-Saxon/Scandinavian)

- Landholding warrior-noble who served the king directly
- Similar to a knight

**Lendmann** (Norwegian)

- Nobles who held land from the king
- Provided military service with a certain number of warriors

**Hersir** (Norwegian)

- Local military commander and administrator, below a jarl
- Led local forces and collected taxes

---- 

## Special Ecclesiastical Position

**Vidame** (French)

- Secular representative of a bishop
- Managed the bishop’s temporal affairs and military obligations
